## Voice 1 — Reviewer 1 (Physics correctness)

This manuscript presents a compelling hybrid approach to Hamiltonian simulation that combines Trotter formulas with linear combination of unitaries (LCU) compensation. The physical setup is well-grounded in the standard formalism of quantum simulation, where the Hamiltonian is decomposed as H = Σ Hₗ with each summand being a weighted Pauli operator. The authors correctly identify the fundamental tension between Trotter methods, which enjoy commutator scaling and near-linear system-size dependence for lattice Hamiltonians but suffer from polynomial accuracy scaling, and post-Trotter methods, which achieve logarithmic accuracy dependence but lose the favorable locality structure. The physical intuition that one should "let Trotter handle the majority of the simulation, with LCU completing the remainder" is sound and represents a natural interpolation between these regimes.

The treatment of the Trotter remainder VK(x) = U(x)SK(x)† and its Taylor series expansion is mathematically rigorous. The authors correctly exploit the order conditions established in prior work, particularly the vanishing of expansion terms FK,1 through FK,K, which follows from the well-known property that Trotter formulas match the exact evolution to order K in the time step. The observation that the leading-order terms FK,K+1 through FK,2K+1 are anti-Hermitian is crucial for the pairing construction, and the proof via the BCH formula in Equation 69 is correct. However, I have concerns about the treatment of lattice Hamiltonians in Section V. The authors define H = A + B where A contains even-indexed terms and B contains odd-indexed terms, but the boundary conditions are not clearly specified. For periodic boundary conditions, the counting arguments in Proposition 7 need modification, and the "light cone" analysis in Figure 6 would need to wrap around. The authors should clarify whether they assume open boundary conditions throughout.

The nested commutator bounds in Proposition 12 appear correct, but the constants involved deserve scrutiny. The factor β = 2(4κ + 5), where κ is the Trotter stage number, grows as 2(4 × 2 × 5^(k-1) + 5) for 2k-th order formulas, which becomes substantial for higher orders. While the authors focus on first- and second-order cases in their numerics, the scaling of these prefactors matters for practical applicability. The treatment of the Heisenberg model in Equation 20 as a test case is appropriate, with the decomposition in Equation 21 ensuring Λ₁ = 4 for uniform sampling. The padding technique introduced in Equations 17-19 to ensure uniform 1-norms across elementary commutators is clever and preserves the locality structure, but the virtual qubit construction in Figure 6 needs more careful justification. Specifically, when the light cone reaches the boundary after s adjoint operations, adding virtual qubits with ±I operations maintains the algebraic structure but may introduce subtle issues in the sampling probability normalization that are not fully addressed.

The random sampling implementation in Section II.D is physically sound, with the Hadamard test circuit in Figure 9 providing an unbiased estimator of Tr(OVρV†). The μ⁴ overhead in the sample complexity (Proposition 1) is correctly identified as the key cost of the random sampling approach, and maintaining μ as a constant is essential for efficiency. The authors' claim that the algorithm achieves "2 orders of magnitude smaller" gate counts than fourth-order Trotter for generic Hamiltonians and "3 to 4 orders of magnitude higher accuracy" for lattice systems with the same gate costs requires verification against specific parameter regimes. The numerical results in Figure 8 support these claims, but the comparison methodology deserves scrutiny: the fourth-order Trotter bounds are analytical (from Ref. [12]) while the PTSC results appear to be computed from the LCU formula construction. This asymmetry could favor the Trotter-LCU approach. I recommend the authors provide a comparison using both analytical bounds and empirical circuit depths for all methods.

Questions for Authors: First, can you clarify the boundary conditions assumed for the lattice Hamiltonian analysis, and if periodic boundaries are intended, how do the light-cone arguments in Section V.B need to be modified? Second, for the Heisenberg model results in Figure 8(c), what is the specific procedure for computing the "fourth-order Trotter commutator bound" and can you confirm it uses the nested commutator form from Proposition M.1 of Ref. [20] rather than cruder estimates? Third, the virtual qubit padding construction appears to introduce extra terms in the LCU formula even though they contribute zero to the final result; does this affect the variance of the random sampling estimator, and if so, is this accounted for in your sample complexity analysis?

Verdict: 7/10. Recommendation: minor-revisions.

## Voice 2 — Reviewer 2 (Algorithmic novelty)

The central algorithmic contribution of this work is the observation that LCU compensation of Trotter remainders can achieve better-than-2K-th order scaling using only K-th order Trotter formulas. This is a genuine insight that has not appeared in the literature in precisely this form. However, the landscape of Hamiltonian simulation has evolved rapidly, and the novelty must be assessed against recent developments. The random sampling implementation of LCU, which the authors credit to Refs. [30, 33-35], has been substantially developed in works by Wan, Berta, and Campbell (Ref. [34], PRL 2022) and Faehrmann et al. (Ref. [35], Quantum 2022). The specific contribution here is the construction of LCU formulas that exploit Trotter order conditions and the pairing technique using Euler's formula, neither of which appears in prior random-LCU work. This constitutes a novel algorithmic element.

The comparison to the most recent literature reveals both strengths and gaps. The work by Childs, Ostrander, and Su on randomized Trotter (Ref. [12], Quantum 2019) achieves improved practical performance through random permutation of Trotter stages, but maintains the polynomial accuracy scaling. The authors correctly identify that their approach achieves time scaling of 1 + 1/(2K+1) compared to 1 + 1/K for standard K-th order Trotter, and logarithmic accuracy scaling for PTSC. However, I note that very recent work by Cho, Berry, and Hsieh (Ref. [47], PRA 2024, cited in the manuscript) considers "similar ideas to compensate the Trotter error using randomized unitary operators." The authors acknowledge this in Section VI but do not provide a detailed comparison. This is a significant oversight given that Ref. [47] appeared on arXiv in the same timeframe as earlier versions of this manuscript. A more thorough comparison establishing the precise relationship and relative advantages would strengthen the novelty claim.

The algorithmic structure involves several components whose individual novelty varies. The Taylor series expansion of Trotter remainders is straightforward, and the observation that leading-order terms are anti-Hermitian follows from standard BCH analysis. The true novelty lies in the pairing construction (Equations 45 and 75) and the nested commutator compensation for lattice Hamiltonians. The pairing technique essentially converts additive error terms of the form I + iηV into rotation unitaries exp(iθV), reducing the 1-norm from 1 + η to √(1 + η²). This is elegant but also somewhat obvious once stated; the key question is whether it constitutes a "strict domination" improvement over prior work. Examining the complexity claims in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, we have gate complexity O((λt)^(1+1/(2K+1))(κK L + log(1/ε)/loglog(1/ε))) for PTSC and O(n^(1+2/(2K+1)) t^(1+1/(2K+1)) ε^(-1/(2K+1))) for NCC on lattice Hamiltonians. These should be compared against the best known results: O((λt)^(1+1/K) ε^(-1/K)) for K-th order Trotter, O(n² t log(1/ε)) for post-Trotter methods on generic Hamiltonians, and O(n^(1+1/K) t^(1+1/K) ε^(-1/K)) for K-th order Trotter on lattice Hamiltonians. The PTSC algorithm achieves a strict Pareto improvement in the (t, ε) parameter space for fixed system size, and NCC achieves a Pareto improvement in the (n, t, ε) space for lattice systems. These are legitimate improvements, though the practical margins depend strongly on prefactors.

The claims about orders of magnitude improvements (2 orders for generic Hamiltonians, 3-4 orders of accuracy for lattice systems) require careful verification. From Figure 8(a), at t = 10² and ε = 10⁻⁵, the fourth-order Trotter gate count is approximately 10¹⁰ while fourth-order PTSC is approximately 10⁸, yielding a ratio of about 100×, consistent with the "2 orders of magnitude" claim. From Figure 8(c), at the same gate count (approximately 10⁶ Rz gates), fourth-order Trotter achieves ε ≈ 10⁻³ while second-order NCC achieves ε ≈ 10⁻⁷, a ratio of 10⁴ in accuracy, consistent with the "3-4 orders of magnitude" claim. These ratios are indeed achievable but occur in specific parameter regimes; the claims should be qualified accordingly. The authors should also note that the comparison uses analytical bounds for Trotter, which are known to be loose by constant factors in practice.

Questions for Authors: First, can you provide a detailed comparison with Ref. [47] (Cho, Berry, Hsieh) establishing precisely which algorithmic elements are shared and which are novel to your work? Second, for the claimed "2 orders of magnitude" improvement, can you compute the gate counts using empirical Trotter error estimates (e.g., from numerical simulation) rather than analytical bounds to provide a more balanced comparison? Third, the transition from PTSC to NCC occurs when locality structure becomes important; can you characterize more precisely the crossover point in the (n, L, t, ε) parameter space where NCC becomes preferable to PTSC? Fourth, have you considered combining your approach with randomized Trotter (Ref. [12]) to potentially achieve further improvements?

Verdict: 6/10. Recommendation: major-revisions.

## Voice 3 — Reviewer 3 (Empirical evidence)

The numerical evidence presented in this manuscript centers on gate count comparisons in Figure 8 and the resource estimation framework described in Section II.E and Appendix G. While the theoretical complexity results are rigorously derived, the empirical validation raises several methodological concerns that must be addressed before publication. The gate counting procedure is described as follows: circuits are compiled to CNOT gates, single-qubit Clifford gates, and non-Clifford Rz(θ) gates, with T-gate counts derived using the compilation formula from Ref. [54] with cT ≈ 66 T gates per Rz gate at precision 10⁻¹⁵. This is a reasonable procedure, but the manuscript does not provide confidence intervals or variance estimates for any of the reported gate counts. Given that the PTSC and NCC algorithms involve random sampling, the gate counts are random variables whose distributions should be characterized.

For the random sampling implementation, the sample complexity is O(μ⁴/ε²) where μ is the LCU 1-norm and ε is the estimation precision (Proposition 1). The authors set μ = 2 for the comparisons in Figure 8, which implies a 16× overhead in sample number. However, the plots show single-shot gate counts rather than total resource requirements including the sampling overhead. This is explicitly acknowledged ("the random-sampling Trotter-LCU algorithm will require an additional factor of 16 in the sample number"), but it would be more informative to present total gate counts including the sampling repetitions required to achieve a target observable estimation precision. Furthermore, the claim that PTSC achieves "2 orders of magnitude smaller" gate counts assumes that the fourth-order Trotter analytical bounds are tight. The authors use bounds from Ref. [12] for randomized Trotter, but it is well-known that analytical Trotter error bounds can overestimate the true error by orders of magnitude. A more rigorous comparison would include numerical estimates of the actual Trotter error for specific Hamiltonians.

The ablation study structure is incomplete. The manuscript presents results for 0th-order PTSC, 2nd-order PTSC, 4th-order PTSC, and 2nd-order NCC, but does not systematically vary other algorithmic choices. Key questions include: what is the effect of the truncation order sc on practical performance, how does the pairing technique compare to direct compensation without pairing, and what is the sensitivity to the padding procedure in the NCC algorithm? The authors mention in Section VI that "we can think about a hybrid implementation of different algorithms" but do not provide any numerical evidence for such hybrid approaches. A complete ablation would also include the effect of measurement and reset versus maintaining the ancilla qubit coherently (as discussed in Figure 10), which affects the circuit depth and connectivity requirements differently.

The absence of a systematic failure modes analysis is concerning. The manuscript does not discuss regimes where the Trotter-LCU approach may perform poorly. For instance, when the LCU 1-norm μ becomes large (which occurs when the leading-order Trotter error terms have substantial norm), the sample complexity grows as μ⁴. The authors state that μ can be kept constant by increasing the segment number ν, but this trades off against circuit depth. There should be a discussion of cases where this tradeoff becomes unfavorable. Additionally, the numerical results focus on specific model Hamiltonians (the 2-local Hamiltonian in Equation G2 and the Heisenberg model in Equation 20), but no systematic study of Hamiltonian families is provided. The claim that the approach is "generic for L-sparse Hamiltonians" should be substantiated with results across a broader class of systems. Finally, there is no mention of an audit trail or reproducibility infrastructure. The manuscript does not reference any code repository, supplementary data files, or scripts that would allow independent verification of the numerical claims. For a paper making quantitative resource estimation claims, this is a significant deficiency.

Questions for Authors: First, can you provide variance estimates or confidence intervals for the gate counts shown in Figure 8, accounting for the randomness in the sampling procedure? Second, can you include numerical estimates of actual Trotter error (not just analytical bounds) for the Hamiltonians studied, to provide a more balanced comparison with your method? Third, what is the performance of your algorithms on quantum chemistry Hamiltonians, which you mention as a potential application but do not study numerically? Fourth, will you provide a code repository with scripts to reproduce all numerical results, including the gate count calculations and the LCU formula construction?

Verdict: 5/10. Recommendation: major-revisions.

## Voice 4 — Devil's Advocate

This manuscript, despite its mathematical sophistication, fails to deliver on its central promise of providing practical algorithms for near-term Hamiltonian simulation. Let me systematically dismantle the claims.

The foundational premise that these algorithms are "easy to implement" is misleading at best. The authors repeatedly contrast their approach with "post-Trotter" methods that require "multicontrolled Toffoli gates" and "many ancillary qubits." But examining their own construction reveals substantial hidden complexity. The PTSC algorithm requires sampling from a distribution over Taylor expansion orders up to sc, followed by sampling Pauli strings based on multinomial distributions with O(L) outcomes per trial. For each sampled configuration, one must compute the Pauli rotation angle θ = tan⁻¹(ηΣ) where ηΣ depends on the full expansion structure. The NCC algorithm is worse: it requires traversing "light cone" regions in the commutator structure, sampling adjoint operators with carefully computed probabilities, and handling boundary padding with virtual qubits. Algorithm 2 in Appendix D spans two pages of pseudocode. Calling this "easy to implement" when compared to, say, a straightforward fourth-order Trotter circuit that requires no classical preprocessing is disingenuous.

The claimed resource advantages evaporate under closer scrutiny. The authors proudly state "2 orders of magnitude smaller" gate counts, but this comparison is fundamentally unfair. They compare their algorithms against the analytical worst-case bound for fourth-order Trotter from Ref. [12], which is known to overestimate the actual error by substantial margins. In contrast, the Trotter-LCU gate counts appear to be computed from the explicit LCU formula construction, essentially a best-case estimate. A fair comparison would use empirical error estimates for both methods. Furthermore, the random sampling implementation incurs a μ⁴ overhead in sample complexity. With μ = 2, this is a factor of 16 in the number of circuit repetitions. The authors bury this fact, mentioning it only in passing, but it means that the "2 orders of magnitude" reduction in per-circuit gate count translates to at most a factor of 100/16 ≈ 6× reduction in total gate resources. This is a much more modest improvement that may not justify the algorithmic complexity.

The nested commutator compensation algorithm, presented as enabling near-optimal system-size scaling for lattice Hamiltonians, has severe limitations that are not adequately discussed. The performance guarantee in Theorem 2 applies only to one-dimensional lattice Hamiltonians of the form H = Σⱼ Hⱼ,ⱼ₊₁. For two-dimensional lattices, which are far more relevant for condensed matter physics and quantum chemistry, the "light cone" structure changes dramatically, and the O(n) commutator bounds become O(n^d) for d-dimensional systems. The authors never explicitly state this limitation. Additionally, the NCC algorithm only compensates the leading-order terms (order K+1 through 2K+1), leaving a residual error of O(x^(2K+2)). This means the accuracy scaling is ε^(-1/(2K+1)), which is polynomial rather than logarithmic. The PTSC algorithm achieves logarithmic accuracy scaling but loses the favorable system-size dependence. The authors present this as a choice between two advantageous regimes, but in reality, neither algorithm achieves both simultaneously, which is the actual holy grail of Hamiltonian simulation.

The numerical results are cherry-picked to favor the proposed methods. Figure 8(a) shows time dependence at fixed n = 20 and ε = 10⁻⁵, a regime deliberately chosen to showcase the superior time scaling of PTSC. At shorter times (t < 10), the methods are comparable. Figure 8(c) uses n = t for the lattice Hamiltonian, which obscures the separate contributions of system size and evolution time. Most damningly, the authors do not compare against quantum signal processing (QSP) in the main text results for lattice Hamiltonians, despite QSP being the state-of-the-art for generic Hamiltonians. The comparison with QSP in Appendix G shows that PTSC has higher T-gate counts than QSP (Figure 14), completely undermining the "easy to implement" narrative. On fault-tolerant quantum computers where T gates dominate the cost, QSP remains superior. The authors try to salvage this by noting PTSC has lower CNOT counts and qubit requirements, but these are secondary considerations for fault-tolerant architectures.

This paper represents solid theoretical work that has been oversold as a practical breakthrough. The algorithms are mathematically elegant but algorithmically complex, the resource advantages are overstated through unfair comparisons, and the fundamental limitations are underemphasized. I recommend rejection unless the authors substantially revise their claims and provide balanced comparisons using empirical error estimates for all methods.

Recommendation: reject.

## Voice 5 — Editor-in-Chief synthesis

Having reviewed all four assessments, I find substantial merit in this manuscript alongside significant concerns that must be addressed. Let me reconcile the conflicting perspectives.

Reviewer 1 raises valid technical points about boundary conditions and the asymmetry in comparison methodologies but ultimately finds the physics correct and the core construction sound. Reviewer 2 acknowledges genuine algorithmic novelty in the pairing technique and nested commutator compensation but correctly identifies the missing comparison with concurrent work (Ref. [47]) as a significant gap. Reviewer 3's concerns about empirical methodology are well-founded: the lack of confidence intervals, incomplete ablation studies, and absence of reproducibility infrastructure are problematic for a paper making quantitative resource claims. The Devil's Advocate raises the most serious challenges: the "easy to implement" framing is oversold, the comparison methodology favors the proposed methods, and the practical advantage after accounting for sampling overhead is much smaller than advertised.

On the question of novelty, I side largely with Reviewer 2's assessment. The pairing construction using Euler's formula and the nested commutator compensation for lattice Hamiltonians represent genuine contributions that extend beyond straightforward combinations of known techniques. However, the relationship to Ref. [47] must be clarified explicitly. The authors acknowledge this work in Section VI but dismiss it with the phrase "similar ideas"; this is insufficient for a venue like PRX Quantum where establishing priority and precise technical distinctions is essential.

The Devil's Advocate's critique of the comparison methodology is largely valid but somewhat overstated. The authors do use analytical bounds for Trotter, which are conservative, but this is standard practice in the literature, and the same bounds are used in papers like Ref. [12] and Ref. [20]. A fairer criticism is that the μ⁴ sampling overhead should be more prominently displayed. The authors mention this in Section III.B, but the gate count figures in Section II.E do not include this overhead, which could mislead readers about total resource requirements. That said, the Devil's Advocate's claim that the advantage reduces to "approximately 6×" incorrectly conflates gate counts with sample counts; the total gate count is still reduced even accounting for increased sampling, just not by two orders of magnitude.

For PRX Quantum, which requires both significant scientific advance and thorough technical presentation, this manuscript falls short in its current form but can be elevated with targeted revisions. The core ideas are sound and represent a meaningful contribution to the Hamiltonian simulation literature. The main deficiencies are in presentation and benchmarking methodology rather than in the underlying science.

Must-fix items before resubmission, ordered by severity:

1. Provide an explicit, detailed comparison with Ref. [47] (Cho, Berry, Hsieh), establishing precisely which algorithmic elements are shared, which are novel, and quantifying relative performance where applicable.

2. Revise the resource comparison methodology to include either (a) empirical Trotter error estimates for the test Hamiltonians to match the precision of the LCU formula construction, or (b) analytical bounds for both methods computed using the same framework.

3. Present total resource counts including sampling overhead prominently in the main text, either as separate figures or as multipliers applied to the single-shot counts.

4. Add a Failure Modes section discussing regimes where the approach performs poorly, including quantitative guidance on the (n, L, t, ε) parameter crossovers.

5. Clarify boundary condition assumptions throughout the lattice Hamiltonian analysis and state explicitly that the NCC complexity bounds apply to one-dimensional systems.

6. Provide a reproducibility package (code repository or supplementary materials) containing the scripts and data files necessary to regenerate all numerical results.

7. Add variance or confidence interval estimates to the gate count results, or justify why the quantities reported are deterministic.
